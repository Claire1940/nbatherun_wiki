## Core roles every winning lineup needs

Think in roles first, names second. Whether you run a “Chicago grit” setup, “L.A. pace” setup, or hybrid style, you need each core function covered.

### The five-function model

1. **Primary Engine** – initiates offense under pressure  
2. **Secondary Connector** – keeps possessions alive, swings advantages  
3. **Pressure Defender** – guards point of attack and triggers transition  
4. **Interior Anchor** – handles rim deterrence and board security  
5. **Floor Bender** – stretches help defense and punishes over-rotations

| Role | Primary Job | Must-Have Traits | Common Build Error |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary Engine** | Create efficient first action | Handle security, burst, passing vision | Too much shot bias, low playmaking |
| **Secondary Connector** | Stabilize possessions | Quick reads, off-ball movement | Redundant with primary creator |
| **Pressure Defender** | Disrupt entry actions | Lateral speed, stamina, anticipation | Offensive tunnel vision |
| **Interior Anchor** | Protect paint and glass | Positioning, timing, strength | Slow recovery in switch scenarios |
| **Floor Bender** | Warp spacing | Catch-and-shoot threat, relocation | Low defensive utility |

When building NBA The Run squads, target role overlap only where it increases flexibility. For example, a Secondary Connector who can also defend at point of attack is premium value.

> **💡 Tip:** Draft your starting five with one “non-negotiable skill” per role (e.g., elite outlet passing, elite corner 3, elite rim contests). This keeps your lineups coherent.

## Progression roadmap: from casual lineup to tournament-ready squads

A strong roster is built in layers. Don’t try to max everything at once. Prioritize mechanics that change possession quality first.

| Progression Phase | Main Goal | Focus Upgrades | Success Metric |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Phase 1: Foundation** | Reduce empty possessions | Ball security, passing speed, stamina | Fewer live-ball turnovers |
| **Phase 2: Identity** | Establish reliable scoring pattern | Screening, shooting consistency, spacing | 2 go-to actions per game |
| **Phase 3: Defense** | Stop momentum runs | Point-of-attack defense, rebounding | Fewer 8-0 opponent bursts |
| **Phase 4: Adaptation** | Beat varied opponents | Bench utility, matchup substitutions | Better Game 2 performance |
| **Phase 5: Endgame** | Close tight games | Free throws, late-clock playbook | Higher close-game win rate |

### Weekly improvement loop (practical)

1. **Review 3 losses** and tag why each run failed (turnovers, rebounding, spacing).  
2. **Adjust one lineup variable** only (role swap, bench change, defensive assignment).  
3. **Play 5 test games** using the same priority focus.  
4. **Track one hard stat** (e.g., transition points allowed).  
5. **Keep or revert** based on measurable change.

This loop helps NBA The Run squads improve without constant overhauls that break chemistry.

## Common squad-building mistakes and quick fixes

Even skilled players lose value through preventable roster errors. Fix these first before chasing advanced tactics.

| Mistake | In-Game Symptom | Quick Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Too many on-ball creators** | Stalled spacing, forced isolations | Replace one creator with connector wing |
| **No interior anchor** | Easy rim runs, second-chance points | Add paint deterrence specialist |
| **Poor stamina profile** | 4th-quarter drop-off | Rotate bench earlier, optimize minutes |
| **One-way shooters** | Defensive targeting | Pair with strong POA defender |
| **No backup initiator** | Offense collapses under pressure | Train secondary handler reps |

### Bench logic that wins series

Your bench should do one of two things:

- **Maintain structure** when starters rest, or  
- **Change game texture** (more pace, more size, more pressure)

Avoid “random talent” benches that lack purpose. In 2026 competitive settings, purposeful rotations outperform flashy substitutes.

> **⚠️ Warning:** Do not build your second unit as a weaker copy of your starters. Give it a distinct identity so it can solve different matchup problems.

## FAQ

**Q: What are the best NBA The Run squads for beginners in 2026?**

A: Start with a balanced lineup: one primary creator, one reliable shooter, one perimeter stopper, one mobile forward, and one interior anchor. Avoid complex dual-creator systems until your passing reads and defensive rotations feel consistent.

**Q: How many lineup presets should I keep for NBA The Run squads?**

A: Keep at least two presets: a comfort lineup and a counter lineup. If you play tournaments or long sets, a third situational lineup (extra size or extra pace) is useful for specific opponents.

**Q: Should I prioritize star players or chemistry when building NBA The Run team squads?**

A: Prioritize chemistry first, then layer star power. A role-complete team with clean spacing and defensive assignments usually performs better than a star-heavy lineup with overlap and weak support roles.

**Q: How do I know if my NBA The Run squads are improving?**

A: Track practical metrics over 10-game blocks: turnovers, transition points allowed, rebound margin, and close-game record. If those move in the right direction, your squad structure is working even before your win rate fully catches up.
